# Query Explanations

## Query title: Completed revenue calculation
File: basic_aggregations.sql

Business question: How much revenue was generated only from completed orders?
Tables used: orders and products
Why JOIN is needed: The orders table contains the quantity and product_id, but the product price is stored in the products table. The JOIN connects each order with its product price so the revenue can be calculated using quantity * price.
Why WHERE is needed: Only orders with status = 'completed' should be included because completed orders represent actual sales. Pending and cancelled orders do not generate real revenue.
Why GROUP BY is not needed: The goal is to calculate one total revenue value for all completed orders, not separate results for different groups.
What I understood: I learned that revenue cannot be calculated only from the orders table because the price information belongs to the products table. By joining the tables and filtering completed orders, I can calculate the real business revenue.

## Query title: Total quantity ordered by completed orders
File: basic_aggregations.sql

Business question: How many items have customers ordered from completed orders?
Tables used: orders
Why JOIN is not needed: The quantity and status information are already stored in the orders table, so no additional table is required.

Why GROUP BY is not needed: The query calculates the total quantity across all completed orders and returns one summary value.

## Query title: Potential value from pending and cancelled orders
File: basic_aggregations.sql

Business question: What is the possible value of orders that are not completed yet or were cancelled?
Tables used: orders and products
Why JOIN is needed: The orders table has the ordered quantity, while the products table contains the product price. Both values are needed to calculate the potential value using quantity * price.
Why WHERE is needed: The query only considers pending and cancelled orders because these orders are not completed sales.
Why GROUP BY is needed: The results should show separate potential values for each status, so the query groups the data by pending and cancelled statuses.
What I understood: I learned that potential value is different from real revenue. Pending orders might still become sales, but cancelled orders will not generate income, so they should not be included in completed revenue reports.

Business question: Which products generated the highest revenue from completed orders?

## Query title: Revenue calculation by order status
File: group_by_reports.sql

Business question: How much value is associated with each order status?
Tables used: orders and products
Why JOIN is needed: The orders table stores the quantity and status of each order, while the products table stores the product price. The JOIN allows the query to calculate the value of orders for each status.

Why GROUP BY is needed: The results are grouped by status to compare the calculated value between completed, pending, and cancelled orders.
What I understood: I learned that this report is useful for understanding order distribution, but it is not always a good revenue report because cancelled orders are included even though they do not create real income. Completed revenue should only include successful sales.

## Query title: Customers with more than one order
File: group_by_reports.sql

Business question: Which customers have placed more than one order?
Tables used: orders
Why JOIN is not needed: All required information is available in the orders table because the query only needs customer_id and the number of orders.
Why WHERE is not needed: The query does not filter individual rows before counting. Instead, it checks the grouped results after counting.
Why GROUP BY is needed: The query groups orders by customer_id to calculate how many orders each customer has made.
Why HAVING is needed: HAVING is used because the condition COUNT(*) > 1 applies to grouped results, not individual rows. It filters only customers whose total number of orders is greater than one.
What I understood: I learned the difference between WHERE and HAVING. WHERE filters rows before grouping, while HAVING filters groups after aggregate functions like COUNT() are calculated.

## Query title: Top 3 customers by completed revenue
File: join_reports.sql

Business question: Which three customers generated the highest revenue from completed orders?
Tables used: orders, customers, and products
Why JOIN is needed: The orders table contains customer_id, product_id, and quantity. The customers table is needed to display customer names, while the products table provides the price required to calculate the revenue.
Why WHERE is needed: The query filters only completed orders because cancelled or pending orders do not represent actual customer revenue.
Why GROUP BY is needed: The results are grouped by customer_name so the total completed revenue can be calculated separately for each customer.
Why LIMIT is needed: LIMIT 3 is used because the business question asks only for the top three customers with the highest completed revenue.
What I understood: I learned how to create a ranking report by combining multiple tables, calculating totals with SUM(), sorting results with ORDER BY, and limiting the output to the most important results.
